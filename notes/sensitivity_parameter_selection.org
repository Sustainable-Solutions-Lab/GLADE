* Parameter selection for global sensitivity analysis

Notes on which parameters to include in the PCE-based global sensitivity
analysis, based on preliminary scenario sweeps (configs =current_yields= and
=prod_stability=).

** Food utility meta-parameters — not relevant

We swept the piecewise utility parameters =decline_factor= ∈ {0.5, 0.7, 0.9}
and =n_blocks= ∈ {2, 4, 8} in a 3×3 grid, both with and without health/GHG
pricing. Global average diets are nearly identical across all 9 combinations in
both pricing regimes. The model is robust to these meta-parameters; they do not
need to be included in the sensitivity analysis.

** Production stability L1 penalty — potentially relevant

We swept =l1_cost= across {0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.5, 1, 5, 10, 50, 100} in two
pricing regimes: (yll=5000, ghg=200) and (yll=0, ghg=0).

- *With health/GHG pricing*: Diets are stable across the full range of L1
  costs. The pricing signals dominate the solution structure.
- *Without pricing*: L1 cost has a strong effect on diet composition. Low
  values allow the optimizer to deviate freely from baseline production; high
  values lock in current patterns.

This suggests L1 cost interacts with the pricing parameters and could be worth
including, at least as a nuisance parameter or to verify it doesn't confound the
main effects.

** Current PCE sensitivity parameters

The =pce_sensitivity.yaml= config currently includes the following parameters.
See =docs/sensitivity_analysis.rst= for full justification with references.

*Emission factors* (currently active):
- =ch4_factor= [0.5, 1.5]: ±50%, representing combined EF + GWP100
  uncertainty. EF uncertainty ±40% (midpoint of IPCC Tier 1 ±30–50%)
  and GWP100 uncertainty ±40% (IPCC AR6, 90% CI) combine in quadrature
  to ±57%, rounded conservatively to ±50%.
- =n2o_factor= [0.3, 1.7]: ±70%, representing combined EF + GWP100
  uncertainty. EF uncertainty ±50% (Tian et al. 2020 global aggregate
  ×0.66–1.53; structural uncertainty from aggregated EFs) and GWP100
  uncertainty ±47% (IPCC AR6, 90% CI) combine in quadrature to ±69%,
  rounded to ±70%.
- =luc_factor= [0.3, 1.7]: ±70%, matching IPCC AR6 WGIII 90% CI for
  CO2-LULUCF emissions. Supported by Global Carbon Budget CV ≈ 58% (1σ),
  AGB uncertainty ~50%, and SOC factor uncertainty ~20–25%.

*Crop yields* (currently active):
- =yield_factor= [0.8, 1.2]: ±20% global crop yield uncertainty. Well-
  supported by grid-cell NRMSE (18–28%) and inter-annual CV (13–22% for
  major cereals). Directly drives land_use and ghg_emissions (through LUC).

*Health relative risk quantiles* (currently active):
- =rr_whole_grains= [0, 1]: Interpolates between GBD low/high RR estimates.
- =rr_legumes= [0, 1]: Same.
- =rr_red_meat= [0, 1]: Same.

*Valuation / slice parameters* (currently active):
- =value_per_yll= [0, 20000]: YLL monetary valuation (policy choice).
- =ghg_price= [0, 300]: Carbon price (policy choice).

*Not currently included*:
- =rr_fruits=, =rr_vegetables=, =rr_nuts_seeds=: Additional health risk
  factors. Left out to keep dimensionality manageable; these food groups may
  be less decision-relevant than grains/legumes/red meat.

** Emission factor coverage

The =ch4_factor= and =n2o_factor= sensitivity parameters must scale ALL
emission sources of each gas, not just animal production. In the baseline
(validation) scenario, the emission breakdown is:

| Source            | MtCO₂eq | % of total | Notes                         |
|-------------------+---------+------------+-------------------------------|
| Animal CH₄       |    3795 |        68% | Enteric + manure              |
| Rice CH₄         |     232 |         4% | Wetland rice cultivation      |
| Animal N₂O       |     958 |        17% | Manure (pasture + managed)    |
| Fertilizer N₂O   |     561 |        10% | Synthetic N application       |

The =ch4_factor= should scale both =animal_production= and =crop_production=
(rice) CH₄ links. The =n2o_factor= should scale both =animal_production= and
=fertilizer_distribution= N₂O links. The GWP100 uncertainty component applies
identically to all sources of the same gas; the EF uncertainty is similar
enough across sources (IPCC Tier 1/2 ranges) to justify a single factor.

** Food loss and waste factor — to be included

*** Rationale

Food loss and waste (FLW) fractions scale how much crop production is needed
per unit of food consumed. The model currently uses SDG 12.3.1 data (food loss
index + food waste index) from FAO/UNEP. These estimates have substantial
uncertainty from measurement methodology, country-level data gaps, and scope
differences.

*** Proposed range: =flw_factor= [0.7, 1.3]

A ±30% range around the current SDG-based estimates is justified by:

- *Inter-estimate disagreement*: Global FLW estimates range from ~24% of food
  supply (Kummu et al. 2012, caloric basis) to ~33% (FAO 2011, mass basis) to
  ~40% (WWF 2021, including on-farm losses). This ×0.73–1.21 range of the
  central estimate reflects scope and metric differences.
- *Measurement bias*: Self-reported consumer waste (which feeds UNEP
  estimates) systematically underestimates actual waste by 20–40% compared to
  direct waste compositional analysis (Quested et al. 2020). This biases the
  SDG data low.
- *Country-level data uncertainty*: The SDG 12.3.1 Food Loss Index has a
  stated random error of ~25% at the country level (FAO 2019). Only ~12% of
  the global population lives in countries that directly track FLW (WRI).
- *Historical data staleness*: Much post-harvest loss data for developing
  countries was collected over 30 years ago (Parfitt et al. 2010), though the
  bias direction is unclear.

A symmetric ±30% range is a conservative choice; the dominant bias direction in
the literature is underestimation, which would support an asymmetric range
skewed higher. We use the symmetric range for simplicity.

*** Key references

- Gustavsson et al. (2011): /Global food losses and food waste/. FAO.
- Kummu et al. (2012). doi:10.1016/j.scitotenv.2012.08.092
- Alexander et al. (2017). doi:10.1016/j.agsy.2017.01.014
- Xue et al. (2017). doi:10.1021/acs.est.7b00401
- FAO (2019): /The State of Food and Agriculture 2019/. FAO.
- Quested et al. (2020). doi:10.1016/j.jclepro.2020.123635
- UNEP (2024): /Food Waste Index Report 2024/. UNEP.
- WWF-UK (2021): /Driven to Waste/. WWF.

*** Implementation

Apply =flw_factor= as a multiplicative scaling of all FLW fractions in
=sensitivity.py=. Since FLW enters the model as part of the efficiency on food
processing links (=food_processing= carrier), the factor should scale the loss
component: =adjusted_efficiency = base_efficiency × (1 + flw_factor × (1 -
base_efficiency) / base_efficiency)= — or equivalently, just scale the
=1 - loss_fraction= term by the factor. Needs design thought on the cleanest
implementation approach.

** Feed conversion ratio factor — to be included

*** Rationale

Feed conversion ratios (FCRs) determine how much feed is needed per unit of
animal product. The model uses Wirsenius (2000) regional feed energy
requirements, converted via NRC NE-to-ME factors. FCR uncertainty propagates to
land use (through feed crop demand), GHG emissions (through animal production
links), and total cost.

*** Proposed range: =fcr_factor= [0.8, 1.2]

A ±20% range is justified by:

- *Inter-source disagreement*: The model applies calibration multipliers (up to
  2.0×) to reconcile Wirsenius-based efficiencies with GLEAM feed baselines.
  After calibration, residual disagreement between Wirsenius and GLEAM/Herrero
  et al. (2013) is typically 10–30% for a given region-product pair.
- *NE-to-ME conversion uncertainty*: The model uses k_m=0.60, k_g=0.40,
  k_l=0.60 (NRC 2000 "typical" values). Published ranges are k_m: 0.55–0.65,
  k_g: 0.35–0.45, introducing ~10–15% uncertainty.
- *Temporal lag*: Wirsenius data reflects ~1994–1998 conditions. Monogastric
  FCRs have improved ~10–20% since then (genetic progress ~0.5–1%/year for
  poultry/pork); ruminant FCR improvement is minimal.
- *Carcass-to-retail factor variation*: 5–10% across sources and countries.
- *Precedent*: Alexander et al. (2016) and Springmann et al. (2018) both used
  ±20% for FCR in Monte Carlo sensitivity analyses. IPCC 2019 Refinement
  reports ±20% uncertainty for Tier 2 feed intake estimates.

The ±20% range captures data source disagreement, conversion factor
uncertainty, and temporal lag without bleeding into inter-system variation
(which is already captured by the model's regionalized FCR assignment).

*** Key references

- Wirsenius (2000). PhD thesis, Chalmers University.
- Herrero et al. (2013). doi:10.1073/pnas.1308149110
- Mottet et al. (2017). doi:10.1016/j.gfs.2017.01.001
- Alexander et al. (2016). doi:10.1016/j.gloenvcha.2016.09.005
- Springmann et al. (2018). doi:10.1038/s41586-018-0594-0
- IPCC (2019): /2019 Refinement/, Vol 4, Ch 10.
- NRC (2000): /Nutrient Requirements of Beef Cattle/, 7th ed.

*** Implementation

Apply =fcr_factor= as a multiplicative scaling of =efficiency= on
=animal_production= links in =sensitivity.py=. Higher factor means better feed
conversion (more product per unit feed), lower factor means worse.

** Open questions

- Should =l1_cost= be added as a parameter? It's not a physical uncertainty but
  a modelling choice; including it would test robustness to this assumption.
  Preliminary sweeps suggest it interacts with pricing parameters.
- The =total_width_multiplier= for food utility was not tested (held at 2.0).
  Given the insensitivity to the other utility parameters, this is likely fine.
- Additional RR factors (fruits, vegetables, nuts_seeds) could be added if
  dimensionality allows. Currently excluded to keep the parameter count
  manageable.
